SLOPE COMPARISON FINDINGS
UMCES Coastal Bays Project — Nutrient Trend Analysis (1995–2008)
=================================================================

WHAT THE SLOPE MEANS
---------------------
The OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) slope represents the average rate of change
per year for a given nutrient in a given coastal bay segment. It is derived
from a linear regression fit to yearly-averaged concentration data.

  - Positive slope = nutrient concentration trending upward over time
  - Negative slope = nutrient concentration trending downward over time
  - Near zero      = relatively stable, no strong directional trend

Units are micromolars per year (chlorophyll in μg/L per year).


WHAT THE CHART SHOWS
---------------------
Each group of bars represents one coastal bay segment (I–VII). Within each
group, one bar is shown per nutrient (phosphate, nitrate, silicate, ammonium,
chlorophyll). This allows two types of comparison simultaneously:

  - Across nutrients within a segment: are all nutrients behaving the same
    way, or do some diverge?
  - Across segments for one nutrient: is a trend bay-wide or localized?

KEY FINDINGS
-------------
1. SILICATE IS RISING FASTEST across nearly all segments. Segment VI shows
   the steepest increase (~0.44 micromolars/year), and the trend holds
   consistently from Segments I through VI. This may indicate changes in
   freshwater input, sediment flux, or shifts in diatom activity across
   the bay.

2. NITRATE IS THE ONLY NUTRIENT DECLINING in some segments (I, III, IV),
   diverging from the upward trend seen in all other nutrients. This could
   reflect localized remediation efforts, different pollution sources, or
   biological uptake patterns specific to those areas.

3. AMMONIUM INCREASES CONSISTENTLY ACROSS ALL SEVEN SEGMENTS with no
   exceptions. This suggests a bay-wide pressure — such as atmospheric
   deposition or widespread agricultural runoff — rather than a source
   localized to any one area.

4. SEGMENT VII IS ANOMALOUS — nearly all nutrients show near-zero slopes,
   behaving fundamentally differently from the rest of the bay. This may
   reflect its geographic position, reduced human impact, or distinct
   hydrological characteristics compared to other segments.


WHY THIS ANALYSIS MATTERS
--------------------------
The individual regression plots (linomial.py) show one nutrient in one
segment at a time. The slope comparison shows the entire system at once,
making cross-segment and cross-nutrient patterns visible that would otherwise
require mentally assembling dozens of individual graphs. It answers not just
"is this nutrient changing?" but "where is it changing, how fast, and does
it match what other nutrients are doing in the same location?"


PRESENTATION SUMMARY (suggested language)
------------------------------------------
"Each bar represents how fast a nutrient was changing per year in a given
segment, based on a linear regression of historical water quality data from
1995 to 2008. A taller bar means a faster rate of increase; a bar going
below zero means that nutrient was declining over the period. This allows us
to compare not just individual nutrients over time, but how different parts
of the bay are responding differently to the same nutrient pressures —
revealing both bay-wide trends and localized anomalies in a single view."
